Qatar and Russia reject criticism of their World Cup winning bids 

Emirate says Garcia report proved it played fair, and Moscow passes challenge back to Fifa

Malcolm Moore in London and Kathrin Hille in Moscow

Wednesday, 3 June, 2015
FIFA

Topics mentioned in this article

  • FIFA
  • Greg Dyke
  • Sepp Blatter
  • Michael Garcia
  • Qatar

Qatar has dismissed speculation it risks being stripped of the 2022 World Cup following Sepp Blatter’s resignation as Fifa president and welcomed a Swiss investigation into how it was awarded the football tournament.

Sheikh Hamad bin Khalifa bin Ahmed al-Thani, president of the Qatar Football Association, said the emirate had already been “cleared of any wrongdoing” by Michael Garcia, a US investigator hired by Fifa to look into the bids.

Russian officials also swiftly dismissed the idea that there could be any threat to the country’s 2018 World Cup hosting rights.

“The 2018 World Cup is in principle not a Russian project, it’s a product of Fifa. Russia offered its services and Fifa agreed,” said Vitaly Mutko, Russia’s sports minister and Fifa executive committee member. “There are no threats here, this is Fifa’s main event, and this is not the decision of one man but of the executive committee,” he told Russian television. “There is no threat whatsoever.”

With Mr Blatter stepping down, there could now be a push by other countries to re-examine the bid processes for Russia and Qatar and award the tournaments to others.

Some observers caution Russia might still be dragged into the investigation. Andrei Piontkovsky, a critic of Vladimir Putin, wrote on Twitter that the Russian president’s congratulations for Mr Blatter after his re-election had been “the kiss of death”.

Alexander Sidyakin, a deputy in Russia’s Duma, suggested there could be a place for Mr Blatter in Russia. “A man with such colossal experience will be in demand in the Russian Football Association during the preparations for the 2018 World Cup in Russia,” he said.

Separately, Interpol on Wednesday issued so-called “red notices” for two former Fifa officials and four corporate executives for charges including racketeering conspiracy and corruption.

A “red notice” informs Interpol member countries that an arrest warrant has been issued for an individual and seeks their location and arrest with a view to extradition or similar action. It is not an international arrest warrant.

The two Fifa officials are Jack Warner, a former vice-president of the organisation, and Nicolás Leoz, a former member of its executive committee.

The shock resignation of Mr Blatter has prompted speculation over who would replace the president of world football’s governing body.

Mr Mutko said Michel Platini, president of the Union of European Football Associations, was a candidate for the succession but “without doubt, there will be others”.

Mr Blatter’s announcement of his departure came after a torrid week in which seven senior Fifa officials were arrested and Swiss police spent a day examining computer files at its Zurich headquarters.

Greg Dyke, chairman of the English Football Association, said minutes after Mr Blatter’s announcement: “If I were Qatar right now I wouldn’t be feeling very comfortable.”

Sheikh Hamad responded: “We welcome the Office of the Swiss Attorney General conducting its own work into the bidding process for the 2018 and 2022 World Cups.

“Mr Dyke’s instinct to focus immediately on stripping Qatar of the World Cup speaks volumes on his views concerning what will be the first Fifa World Cup to take place in the Middle East,” he said.

“We would urge Mr Dyke to let the legal process take its course and concentrate on delivering his promise to build an England team capable of winning the 2022 World Cup in Qatar.”

Mr Garcia’s report has now been passed to the Swiss authorities, who are looking into possible corruption in the bids by Russia and Qatar.

Separately, Qatar has rejected allegations that thousands of migrant workers have died building its World Cup facilities.

“This is completely untrue,” Qatar’s government communications office said in response to the allegations contained in a report in the Washington Post. “In fact, after almost 5m work-hours on World Cup construction sites, not a single worker’s life has been lost. Not one.”

It accused the Washington Post of doing “enormous damage to Qatar’s image” and said it had asked for a retraction.

“In preparing its report, it appears that the Post simply took the total annual mortality figures for Indian and Nepalese migrants working in Qatar and multiplied those numbers by the years remaining between now and the 2022 World Cup — a calculation which assumes that the death of every migrant worker in Qatar is work-related,” it added.
